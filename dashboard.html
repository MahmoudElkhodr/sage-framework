How First-Year Students Report Using AI in Permitted Assessments

Dashboard from task-proximal self-reports collected after a permitted AI assessment

Research Context

The Challenge: Whilst institutional policies on generative AI proliferate across higher education, task-proximal evidence of how students report navigating these tools in authentic assessment contexts remains limited. Assumptions about student behaviour, ranging from concerns about passive use to concerns about uncritical acceptance, often circulate without evidence collected close to actual assessment activity.

What We Studied: This research examined first-year ICT students at Central Queensland University during a supervised in-class assessment where AI use was explicitly permitted under institutional "AI Collaborate" guidelines. Students analysed a peer-reviewed research article whilst having permission to use generative AI tools, then completed an embedded 12-item reflection instrument capturing structured self-reports and open-ended reflections.

How We Collected Data: During Week 12 of Term 3, 2025, 167 students completed structured reflection questions about their reported AI interaction patterns (prompt frequency, verification strategies, revision approaches) and 163 provided qualitative reflections on challenges, support needs, and institutional guidance. The assessment included SAGE-aligned verification prompts requiring cross-referencing with source materials. The dataset does not include prompt logs, screen recordings, grades, or output-quality measures.

Why This Matters: This dashboard presents descriptive evidence of how students reported using AI in one permitted assessment context. The results indicate frequent self-reported verification, revision and iterative interaction, alongside uncertainty about verification adequacy and acceptable-use boundaries. These findings support a partnership-oriented approach to AI literacy development rather than a solely compliance-focused response.

1. Initial Approach Strategies (Q1)
What we asked: How did you begin interacting with the AI tool: conversation style, upload everything at once, or something different for each section?
Meaning Behind the Data: Students most frequently selected conversational interaction (39.5%) and article-first sequential processing (36.5%) as their initial approaches. Only 7.2% selected a monolithic upload-and-extract approach. These self-reports suggest that many students perceived AI interaction as iterative or staged rather than as a single extraction step.

Initial Approach Distribution (Q1)

How students began their AI interaction
Descriptive finding: Conversational (39.5%) and sequential (36.5%) approaches were the two most frequently selected starting strategies, while monolithic upload-and-extract was selected by 7.2% of respondents.
2. Interpretive Behavioural Profiles
What we analysed: Patterns across prompt frequency, verification intensity, and revision strategies were synthesised into four interpretive profiles. These are heuristic categories, not statistical clusters or prevalence groups.
Meaning Behind the Data: Students should not be treated as a homogeneous group. The interpretive profiles provide a vocabulary for discussing different reported orientations to AI use: efficiency-oriented, dialogue-oriented, integrity-oriented, and exploratory. The profiles should guide pedagogical reflection rather than be read as measured clusters.

3. Prompt Frequency Patterns (Q2)
What we asked: How many separate times did you interact with the AI during this assessment—just once, a few times, or many iterations?
Meaning Behind the Data: The most frequently selected prompt-frequency category was 4--8 prompts (55.1%). This indicates repeated but bounded engagement in this assessment context. Prompt frequency was not linked to grades, prompt logs, or output-quality measures, so the bands are interpreted descriptively rather than as evidence of better or worse performance.

Reported number of AI prompts
Descriptive finding: The 4--8 prompt band was the most frequently selected category (55.1%). The prompt bands describe reported interaction intensity only and are not presented as quality indicators.
4. Depth of Engagement: Revision Strategies (Q3)
What we asked: What did you do with AI outputs—use them as-is with minor tweaks, rewrite completely in your own words, or ask follow-up questions for refinement?
Meaning Behind the Data: Many students reported revision-oriented engagement with AI outputs. 81% of students (n=135) selected deeper revision strategies, either rewriting after checking the article (37.7%) or asking follow-up questions (43.1%). Surface-level approaches were less frequently selected (14%, n=24). These are self-reported practices rather than independently observed revision traces.

5. SAGE-Aligned Verification Practices (Q4)
What we asked: How often did you check AI responses against the original article—regularly, occasionally, or rarely?
Meaning Behind the Data: The assessment included SAGE-aligned verification prompts. In this context, 73% of students reported systematic verification (47.9% verified several sections and 25.1% regularly compared outputs against the article). Only 5.4% selected the minimal-verification option. These figures describe declared verification practices within a scaffolded assessment context.

Verification Behaviour (Q4)

Reported source-checking against the article
Descriptive finding: 73% reported systematic verification (47.9% + 25.1%). This is consistent with the relevance of SAGE-aligned verification prompts, but it does not independently verify actual interaction behaviour.
6. The Application Bottleneck (Q5)
What we asked: Which part of the assessment required the most rewriting after receiving AI help—summary, analysis, recommendations, or something else?
Meaning Behind the Data: This item examined which assessment component students most frequently identified as requiring rewriting after AI assistance. Specific recommendations was the most frequently selected component (31.1%), followed by article summary (24.6%). This pattern suggests that context-specific application may require substantial student adaptation.

Assessment Components Requiring Rewriting (Q5)

Where students reported greater rewriting needs
Descriptive finding: Specific recommendations was the most frequently selected component requiring rewriting (31.1%), suggesting that context-specific application required more student adaptation than some other task components.
7. Drivers Beyond Time: Equity and Inclusion (Q7)
What we asked: What factors influenced your decision to use AI—time pressure, English confidence, maintaining your voice, or something else? (Select up to 2)
Meaning Behind the Data: Time available for the assessment was the most frequently selected influence (73.1%), while English writing confidence (46.7%) was the second-highest. In a linguistically diverse cohort, this suggests that some students may perceive AI as a writing-support mechanism rather than only as an efficiency tool.

Top Influencing Factors (Q7)

Why do students turn to AI? (Beyond just "Time")
Equity-relevant finding: English writing confidence (46.7%, n=78) was the second-highest selected influence, suggesting that AI may operate as a perceived linguistic support mechanism for some students.
8. Engagement Patterns: Interaction Strategies (Q8)
What we asked: Which best describes your overall interaction pattern—provided all info at once, built responses step-by-step, or engaged multiple times with verification checks?
Meaning Behind the Data: This question captures the broader reported workflow. 58.1% selected multiple AI interactions interspersed with source checks, while 3.0% selected a comprehensive single-input approach. This pattern is consistent with the value of checkpoint-based engagement design, while remaining based on self-reported practice.

Engagement Pattern Distribution (Q8)

Interaction strategies employed during the assessment
Descriptive finding: Iterative verification was the most frequently selected pattern (58.1%, n=97), followed by incremental building (21.6%, n=36). Comprehensive single-input approaches were selected by 3.0% (n=5).
9. The Friction Points: Technical Challenges (Q9)
What we asked: What was most difficult about using AI effectively—creating useful prompts, verifying accuracy, maintaining your authentic style, or something else? (Select up to 3)
Meaning Behind the Data: Verification of AI accuracy was the most frequently selected challenge (77.8%), followed by maintaining authentic style (64.7%) and producing useful prompts (50.9%). This pattern suggests that students may need support with epistemic judgement and authorial voice, not only with tool operation.

Reported Challenge Hierarchy (Q9)

Most frequently selected difficulties
Descriptive finding: Verifying accuracy (77.8%) and maintaining authentic style (64.7%) were the two most frequently selected challenges. Understanding AI limitations was selected by 16.2%.
10. Retrospective Learning: Strategic Pivot (Q10)
What we asked: If you could redo the assessment, would you change your AI approach—engage more iteratively, verify more thoroughly, or keep the same strategy?
Meaning Behind the Data: This question examined retrospective self-assessment. The most frequently selected future modification was to engage more iteratively (46.7%), followed by using AI more strategically (28.1%) and verifying more thoroughly (25.7%). Only 4.8% selected applying the same approach. Because this item was coded at the option level, these values should be interpreted as selected modification intentions rather than mutually exclusive respondent groups.

Retrospective Pivot (Q10)

"If you could do it again, what would you change?"
Descriptive finding: Engaging more iteratively was the most frequently selected future modification (46.7%, n=78), followed by more strategic AI use and more thorough verification.
11. The Support Wishlist: Student Priorities (Q11)
What we asked: What kind of institutional support would most help you use AI correctly and ethically—verification guidance, prompt examples, practice opportunities, or integrity frameworks?
Meaning Behind the Data: The most frequently selected support need was verification guidance (51.5%), followed by prompt examples (33.5%) and practice opportunities with feedback (29.3%). Integrity frameworks were selected by 19.2%. Because this was an option-level item, these values should not be added as unique respondent totals.

Prioritized Support Needs (Q11)

Specific interventions requested by students
Descriptive finding: Verification guidance (51.5%) and prompt examples (33.5%) were selected more frequently than integrity frameworks (19.2%), suggesting the importance of practical AI-literacy support.

13. Actionable Roadmap: Institutional Recommendations
What this section provides: Based on the empirical evidence from 167 students, this roadmap outlines specific, evidence-based actions institutions can take.
Meaning Behind the Data: Based on the support priorities and reported challenges, this roadmap outlines a phased institutional response. It moves from the immediate need for clarity, to the mid-term need for competency development, and to the long-term need for curricular integration.
Immediate Term (0-3 months)
Policy Clarification and Guidance
  • Publish unified AI policy: Define acceptable use with concrete examples differentiating "idea generation" vs "drafting" vs "editing assistance"
  • Address three ambiguity layers: Definitional (what counts as AI assistance?), procedural (disclosure requirements), evaluative (how will it be assessed?)
  • Establish transparency protocols: Create AI declaration templates for assessments specifying usage levels
  • Communicate institutional stance: Emphasise transparent expectations, developmental support and responsible AI literacy
Mid Term (3-12 months)
Competency Development via Exemplars
  • Develop verification frameworks: Create discipline-specific heuristics for fact-checking AI outputs (51.5% requested this)
  • Build prompt engineering library: Annotated case studies showing effective vs ineffective prompts across assessment types (33.5% demand)
  • Establish practice infrastructure: Consequence-free "sandbox" environments with formative feedback on AI orchestration quality (29.3% requested)
  • Create comparison exemplars: Side-by-side demonstrations of legitimate AI collaboration vs problematic over-reliance
  • Integrate into curriculum: Embed AI literacy explicitly within core units rather than treating as supplementary skill
Long Term (12-24 months)
Assessment Transformation & Infrastructure
  • Transition to process-based assessment: Evaluate AI interaction quality through prompt sequences, verification protocols, and revision strategies rather than output alone
  • Legitimate hybrid authorship models: Develop contribution frameworks explicitly articulating human value-addition in AI-mediated work
  • Emphasize application over extraction: Design assessments requiring context-specific reasoning where AI struggles (recommendations, contextual analysis) rather than information retrieval where AI excels (summarisation)
  • Position students as co-creators: Involve student representatives in AI policy development and revision based on evolving practices
  • Establish AI Support Hub: Centralized resource providing workshops, drop-in consultations, and discipline-specific guidance
14. Key Interpretive Implications
From Data to Interpretation: The reported patterns suggest three interpretive implications for AI literacy, assessment design and institutional support:

Practice-Confidence Tension

Students frequently reported verification while also identifying verification as their main challenge: 73% reported systematic verification, yet 77.8% selected verification accuracy as a difficulty. This pattern suggests:

  • Students may be developing practical AI-use strategies without confidence in their adequacy
  • Uncertainty may arise from unclear assessment boundaries and verification expectations
  • The educational challenge includes formalising and supporting emerging practices
  • Institutions can provide clearer calibration, examples and feedback on responsible AI use

Temporal Reallocation, Not Reduction

Although 73.1% selected time pressure as an influence, students also reported iterative workflows (58.1%) and deeper revision strategies (81%). This suggests:

  • AI adoption may redistribute effort across drafting, checking, revision and contextual application
  • Students report investing effort in verification and revision activities
  • Efficiency motivations can coexist with additional evaluative work
  • Assessment design should make process and verification effort visible

Equity-Relevant Support

English writing confidence was the second-highest selected AI-use influence (46.7%), suggesting that AI may be perceived as a writing-support mechanism by some students:

  • For linguistically diverse cohorts, AI may provide perceived support for academic expression
  • Restrictions on AI use should be assessed for possible equity implications
  • Institutional policies should balance accessibility, authorship, integrity and academic standards
  • AI integration should be considered as both a learning-design and equity-relevant issue
